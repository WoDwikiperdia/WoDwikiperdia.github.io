Augusto Primo Di Diomede (March 14, 1943 - January 2, 2023) was a renowned anthropologist and researcher, known for his extraordinary discoveries in the field of Greek religion.

Biography

Born in Rome, Italy, Augusto Primo Di Diomede was the only child of Onofrio Di Diomede, a renowned Roman lawyer and politician, and Maria del Grillo, a direct descendant of Marquis Onofrio del Grillo, a high-ranking pontifical official of the 18th century. From his early childhood, Augusto displayed an innate curiosity and a passion for studying ancient cultures and their religions. He graduated in Classical Literature from the University of Rome in 1965, completing a thesis on the origins of Greek and Roman mystery cults. Subsequently, he specialized in Archaeology, Anthropology, Philology, Paleontology, and Paleoanthropology, obtaining multiple degrees and doctorates in these disciplines. His Ph.D. in Greek and Latin Mediterranean Anthropology and Religions laid a solid foundation for his future research.On January 2, 2023, at the age of 80, Augusto Primo Di Diomede disappeared under mysterious circumstances while he was heading towards the family chapel in the non-Catholic cemetery of Rome. He had intended to visit his first wife's grave, as he did every year on the anniversary of her death, but he never reached his destination and remained unaccounted for. He was officially declared dead in absentia on January 5, 2023.

Private life

Augusto Primo Di Diomede had a daughter, Elettra Di Diomede, from his first wife, an art historian whose identity has remained private. This first wife always maintained her privacy and never appeared in public. In 2021, Augusto remarried to Nektaria Spanos, a renowned Greek scientist. On January 2, 2023, at the age of 80, Augusto Primo Di Diomede disappeared under mysterious circumstances while heading towards the family chapel in the non-Catholic cemetery of Rome. He had intended to visit the grave of his first wife, as he did every year on the anniversary of her death, but he never reached his destination and remained unaccounted for. He was officially declared dead in absentia on January 5, 2023. His disappearance remains an unsolved enigma in the life of this distinguished scientist.

Studies

Augusto Primo Di Diomede from his early childhood displayed an innate curiosity and a passion for the study of ancient cultures and their religions. He graduated in Classical Literature from the University of Rome in 1965, completing a thesis on the origins of Greek and Roman mystery cults. Subsequently, he specialized in Archaeology at the University of Oxford (United Kingdom), Anthropology at the University of Milan-Bicocca (Italy), Philology at Sapienza University in Rome(Italy), Paleontology at Ludwig Maximilian University in Munich (Germany), and Paleoanthropology at the University of Copenhagen (Denmark), obtaining various degrees and doctorates in these disciplines. His PhD in Mediterranean Greek and Latin Anthropology and Religions , awarded by Sapienza - University of Rome, Department of History, Anthropology, Religions, Art, and Performance - SARAS, formed a solid foundation for his future research.

Scientific Career

Augusto Primo Di Diomede held the position of a professor at the University of Rome, teaching a variety of subjects related to his expertise. He also collaborated with prestigious national and international research institutions, including the National Research Council (CNR), the National Prehistoric Ethnographic Museum "Luigi Pigorini", the National Archaeological Museum of Athens, the British Museum, and the Smithsonian Institution. He not only served as a professor but also acted as a co-advisor for the natural sciences thesis of Angela Albero, a young student who would later achieve great fame as a paleontologist. Additionally, he led numerous archaeological and research expeditions in various parts of the world, including:

  • Explored the presence of mysterious Greek and Roman cults in Anatolia and Turkey, revealing numerous archaeological sites and artifacts of invaluable historical and religious significance.
  • Investigated the Greek and Roman colonization of the Sahara Desert, tracing ancient cities and temples along the trade routes between Africa and the Mediterranean.
  • Made significant discoveries regarding rituals dedicated to Hecate, the goddess of magic, in the Acropolis of Athens, identifying the location where animal sacrifices, spells, and invocations took place.
  • Acted as an archaeological consultant for a study on gender culture in ancient Egypt, analyzing artistic, literary, and religious evidence that unveiled different conceptions of femininity and masculinity in Egyptian society.
  • Syncretic Religion Hecate-Isis: Augusto is known for documenting the earliest presumed example of syncretic religion between Hecate and Isis, a fusion of the Greco-Roman cult of Hecate and the Egyptian cult of Isis. According to his hypothesis, this religion spread among merchants and travelers crossing the Sahara Desert, giving rise to a network of followers and temples dedicated to both goddesses. Augusto published the results of his research in a book titled "Hecate-Isis: A Syncretic Religion between Greece, Rome, and Egypt".
  • Discovery of the Temple of Hecate: He discovered the first temple dedicated to Hecate, the goddess of magic, on the Acropolis of Athens, hidden beneath a more recent structure. This temple was adorned with symbols and images that evoked the attributes and functions of the goddess, such as the moon, dogs, serpents, torches, and keys. The temple also housed an underground altar where secret rituals in honor of Hecate were conducted.
  • Greek Temple in the Desert: His research led to the discovery of the first Greek temple in the Sahara Desert, evidence of Greek cultural influence in what appeared to be a remote and inhospitable region. This temple was dedicated to Hecate-Isis, a syncretic form of the Greek and Egyptian deity of magic. It was located in an oasis, along with a sacred spring and an oracle.
